There is evidence of human settlements in the current Vantaa area from 5000 BC. The Vantaa river valley has had permanent inhabitance since the 14th century and was a part of the Swedish efforts to develop the current Helsinki metropolitan area. The good location regarding trade made the area of high importance, not to mention the traffic enabled by the rivers.
The Russian rule of 1809-1917 saw further improvements to the greater Helsinki area, although the hunger year of 1865 did cause significant loss of life in the region. The first railroad of the country opened in 1862, which enabled areas, such as Tikkurila, to be developed as population centers.
After the independence the greater Helsinki area continued to thrive. Several new population centers were developed in Vantaa after World War 2. The majority of the population were Swedish-speaking in the beginning of the 20th century, but the rapid population growth of the Finnish-speaking population caused them to become the minority.
